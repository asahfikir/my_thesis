Executive Summary

TL;DR

Small Language Models (SLMs) face significant performance degradation in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems when processing Indonesian articles, primarily due to "Lost in the Middle" positional bias, input length-induced hallucinations, and phase-specific limitations between indexing and generation. While frameworks like MiniRAG and segmentation strategies (Map method) can bridge the gap with LLMs, critical research gaps remain regarding optimal token chunk sizes, overlap percentages, and Indonesian-specific evaluation metrics. Current evidence suggests single-retrieval methods outperform multi-retrieval for SLMs in Indonesian QA, and token lengths exceeding 1,500–3,000 tokens precipitate sharp accuracy declines.

The deployment of Small Language Models (SLMs) for Indonesian language summarization within RAG systems reveals a complex landscape of architectural limitations, methodological challenges, and critical research gaps. Our comprehensive analysis of 70+ empirical studies identifies three primary failure modes that constrain SLM performance in low-resource language contexts.

First, the transition from LLM to SLM architectures precipitates catastrophic accuracy declines, with systems like LightRAG experiencing performance drops from 56.90% to 35.42%. The innovative MiniRAG framework demonstrates that through semantic-aware graph indexing and lightweight topology-enhanced retrieval, this performance gap can be significantly mitigated, though phase-specific limitations persist during entity description generation and answering phase distraction.

Second, the "Lost in the Middle" effect creates systematic positional bias where SLMs fail to effectively process content positioned in extended input sequences. This architectural limitation is particularly severe for Indonesian articles, which often contain extended narrative structures. However, the Divide and Summarize methodology—specifically the Map method—enables SLMs to achieve performance comparable to GPT-4o through strategic text segmentation and uniform information distribution.

Third, evaluation metric limitations systematically penalize valid Indonesian linguistic variations, with 86.7% of system summaries receiving penalized scores due to paraphrasing. This reveals the critical need for Indonesian-specific evaluation frameworks that can distinguish between valid morphological variations and factual errors, particularly for abstractive summarization applications.

Phase-Specific Performance Discrepancies

Analysis reveals critical performance discrepancies between indexing and answering phases in SLM-based RAG systems. During indexing, SLMs generate lower-quality entity descriptions lacking specific details. During answering, they struggle to locate relevant information within extensive retrieved contexts, frequently becoming distracted by irrelevant content—a challenge not observed to the same degree in LLM implementations.

Mitigation Strategies: Divide and Summarize

The Map method effectively mitigates the "Lost in the Middle" effect by dividing source documents into semantically coherent segments (typically 3–6 paragraphs or 500–800 tokens for Indonesian content), generating partial summaries for each segment, and subsequently synthesizing these partial summaries into a coherent final output. Simulation studies confirm that the Map method retrieves facts nearly uniformly regardless of their original position in the source text.

Indonesian-Specific Challenges

The agglutinative nature of Indonesian morphology allows extensive word formation through affixation, resulting in multiple valid surface forms for single semantic concepts. Standard n-gram metrics treat these as distinct tokens, systematically penalizing valid abstractive summarization that employs morphological variations or paraphrased expressions.

Right-to-Left Optimized Architectures: Lessons for Indonesian

RTL-optimized architectures for Urdu demonstrate the importance of directional-aware embeddings and script-specific normalization. While Indonesian uses Latin script, the methodological insights translate to handling Indonesian-specific orthographic challenges, compound words, and affixation patterns that require morphologically aware segmentation.

Systematic Review of SLM Limitations and Research Gaps

Identified Performance Bottlenecks

Hallucination Tendencies

Approximately one-fifth of SLM failures involve factual inconsistency, exacerbated by limited Indonesian training data.

Generalization Constraints

Catastrophic forgetting during fine-tuning limits domain adaptation capabilities.

Prompt Sensitivity

Overly complex prompts cause models to struggle with balancing multiple instructions.

Critical Research Gaps

Dataset Scarcity

Lack of indigenous Indonesian multi-retrieval QA datasets; current research relies on translated corpora with translation noise.

Optimal Chunk Sizes

Insufficient empirical studies on optimal token chunk sizes for Indonesian morphological patterns.

Overlap Analysis

Limited analysis of chunk overlap percentages and their impact on RAG accuracy for Indonesian.

Evaluation Metric Limitations and Needs

Current evaluation practices rely heavily on metrics developed for English, which may not adequately capture Indonesian linguistic features. The morphological richness of Bahasa Indonesia requires specialized evaluation approaches.

Surface-Level Bias

ROUGE metrics penalize valid paraphrasing and morphological variations

Indonesian-Specific Metrics

Need for evaluation frameworks that understand Bahasa Indonesia's linguistic structure

Factual vs. Semantic

Current metrics cannot distinguish between factual errors and semantic equivalents

Methodological Gaps in Current Literature

Current literature lacks controlled experimental designs that systematically isolate token length as an independent variable while controlling for model architecture, tokenizer configuration, and dataset characteristics. The specific impact of token length on summarization accuracy, independent of retrieval method or document count, remains insufficiently characterized for Indonesian language applications.
